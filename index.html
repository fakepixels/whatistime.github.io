What is this?

We are fascinated by the concept of time.

The chronological passage of the hours, days, and years on clocks and calendars is a steady, measurable phenomenon designed by humans. Yet our perception of time shifts constantly.

But what does time mean in physics? And how have physicists form and reform the model of time?

We will trace the concept of time all the way back from Aristotle to the recent publication of “The Order of Time” by physicist Carlo Rovelli.

"Time crumbles things; everything grows old under the power of Time and is forgotten through the lapse of Time."

Aristotle

Aristotle is widely referred to as the first Greek philosopher who stated an explicit and concise definition of time.

Aristotle’s Definition



Time is a number interpreted by changes and variation of motion. As such, time is something countable and thus dependent on a mind (or in his words, a soul) that can count.

Time would not exist in the absence of beings who are able to count. Furthermore, there is no change without time and no time without change.

He then goes on to elaborate that things that last forever are not in time, referring to the heavenly bodies that he believes rotate around the earth. This seemingly contradicts his claims that time and change are interdependent because the heavenly bodies are changing but not in time. However, this is justified by the idea that the heavenly bodies are not in time, but rather, surrounded by time.

"Absolute time exists and progresses at a consistent pace throughout the universe."

Newton

Two millennia later, Isaac Newton introduced the concept of absolute time and space.

Newton’s definition



Absolute time exists independently of any perceiver, progresses at a consistent pace throughout the universe, is measurable but imperceptible, and can only be truly understood mathematically.

Most importantly, this concept is consistent with the second law of thermodynamics which states that the total entropy of a system can never be decreased; it is an irreversible process.

Additionally, absolute time and space are two separate aspects of objective reality and independent from physical events. In this model, time is universal in that it is the same for everyone in the universe, but also measured independently of the universe.

In contrast to Aristotle, Newton believed that time would continue even if the universe were void of any matter or objects. His concept of absolute time would go on to provide a theoretical foundation for the classical mechanics that Newton developed to describe the motion of bodies under the influence of forces.

"Time runs differently contingent on relative motion"

Einstein

The modern concept of time was revolutionized by Einstein’s theory of special and general relativity.

Einstein’s definition

Einstein asserted that time runs differently contingent on relative motion. As a result, different observers traveling at different speeds experience unequal passing of time, known as time dilution.

Furthermore, with the introduction of spacetime, the fourth dimension of space, Einstein demonstrated that time can be influenced by gravitational fields to alter the continuum rate of time.

So near a black hole, a clock would appear to tick more slowly and even stop completely at its event horizon to an outside observer so that matter does not actually fall into the black hole.

This explains why our first visual depiction of a black hole has a reddish glow surrounding its event horizon while there is a void at the center.

"Time is just an illusion."

Rovelli

Although Einstein’s concept of time is now considered de facto in academia, a new concept has emerged: the ‘thermal time hypothesis’.

Rovelli’s definition



Rovelli believes that time is just an illusion.

This theory is based on the fact that many equations of quantum gravity are formulated without the reference of time, and time itself “have arisen from aspects of thermodynamics and quantum mechanics.”

Since both fields of physics are bound to principles of quantum uncertainty, our perception regarding time’s flow ultimately depends on entropy, the disorder and lack of predictability of events. If this is not the case then we would not experience the continuous flow of time.
